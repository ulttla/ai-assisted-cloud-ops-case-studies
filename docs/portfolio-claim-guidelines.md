# Public Portfolio Claim Guidelines

## Purpose

Public engineering notes and portfolio pages should be specific enough to be credible, but not so specific that they expose private business details or create claims that are hard to defend later.

This guide generalizes the safer wording patterns used when converting private operational work into public-safe case studies.

## Principle

Prefer claims that are:

- true;
- easy to explain in an interview;
- scoped to the work actually performed;
- free of private customer, account, incident, or infrastructure details;
- supported by a validation result, a case-study boundary, or a clear qualifier.

## Safer rewrite patterns

| Higher-risk wording | Safer public wording |
|---|---|
| Exact budget ownership | Managed infrastructure planning, vendor coordination, and lifecycle budgeting across a lean IT environment |
| Exact savings without context | Reduced platform or operations cost through modernization, with the specific metric used only when evidence-ready |
| Absolute uptime claims | Maintained high service availability or met uptime targets through operational monitoring and standardization |
| Zero incident claims | Completed migration with minimal or no material business disruption, if that wording is accurate |
| Exact mailbox or data volume | Migrated large-scale mail and file data across multiple offices |
| Security incident specifics | Led recovery and resilience improvements after a major security event |
| Exact customer or acquisition details | Supported multi-site integration across a regional business environment |

## When exact numbers are useful

Exact numbers can be powerful when they are:

1. accurate;
2. explainable;
3. scoped;
4. not confidential;
5. not likely to imply a broader guarantee.

For example, a scoped test count in a public case study is usually safer than a broad business result without context.

## Interview-ready test

Before publishing a claim, ask:

- Can I explain how this number was measured?
- Can I describe my personal role without overstating ownership?
- Would the employer, customer, or team be comfortable with this level of detail?
- Does the wording imply a guarantee I cannot support?
- Is there a safer way to preserve the signal without exposing the private detail?

## Good pattern

> Led a cloud migration that improved access and reduced storage cost through SaaS modernization.

This preserves the value without forcing the reader to audit a private budget or invoice trail.

## Public boundary

Do not publish private budgets, customer names, internal incident details, real architecture identifiers, or exact operational data unless the information is already approved for public use and easy to defend.
